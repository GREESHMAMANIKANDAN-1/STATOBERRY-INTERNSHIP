Three-Factor Randomised Block Design (3FRBD)

1Statoberry LLP, 2Department of Agricultural Statistics, Kerala Agricultural University

ABSTRACT

Three-Factor Randomized Block Design (3FRBD) is an advanced experimental design used to evaluate the simultaneous effects of three independent factors and their interactions on one or more response variables, while controlling for extraneous variation through blocking. 3FRBD assigns treatment combinations formed by crossing all levels of three factors randomly within each block, ensuring that environmental or spatial heterogeneity is accounted for across replications. In RAISINS you can perform 3FRBD very easily without writing a single line of code. This tutorial will guide you, how to perform 3FRBD very easily in RAISINS and interpret the results effectively. In addition, you will get tables and plots ready for publication. You can also perform a multivariate analysis including MANOVA and PCA.

Hover or click each point to see more information.

Introduction Three-Factor Randomised Block Design
    The foundations of factorial experimental designs were laid in the 1920s and 1930s by statistician Ronald A. Fisher , who recognized that studying factors one at a time was inefficient and often misleading. Fisher’s pioneering work at Rothamsted Experimental Station demonstrated that crossing multiple factors in a single experiment not only saves resources but also reveals interaction effects that single-factor designs cannot detect. The extension of factorial principles to three factors within a randomized block framework evolved naturally from these contributions, allowing researchers to study the combined effects of, for example, variety, fertilizer level, and irrigation regime simultaneously. The Three-Factor Randomized Block Design (3FRBD) became indispensable in agricultural, biological, and industrial research wherever multiple interacting factors needed to be assessed under field conditions with heterogeneous experimental units. By incorporating blocks to absorb systematic environmental variation, 3FRBD delivers higher precision than a fully randomized factorial arrangement, making it one of the most powerful and widely used designs in experimental science.

2 Three-Factor Randomized Block Design (3FRBD)

A Three-Factor Randomized Block Design (3FRBD) is a factorial experimental design in which all possible combinations of three factors each at two or more levels are arranged within homogeneous blocks, with each complete set of treatment combinations appearing once per block. The design simultaneously evaluates the main effects of each factor and their two-way and three-way interactions, while the blocks absorb variation due to environmental gradients, soil heterogeneity, time, or any other systematic nuisance variable. 3FRBD is widely used in agricultural field trials, greenhouse experiments, pharmaceutical studies, and industrial process optimization wherever multiple interacting factors must be evaluated under conditions that are not fully homogeneous. Its primary advantage over single-factor designs is the ability to detect interaction effects, which are often the most agronomically important findings; its main limitation is the increased number of treatment combinations, which can make block sizes large and difficult to manage when factors have many levels in such cases, incomplete block designs or split-plot arrangements may be more appropriate.

3FRBD Layout

    Figure 2 visually represents a Three-Factor Randomized Block Design arrangement where three factors Factor A (e.g., Variety: a1, a2), Factor B (e.g., Fertilizer: b1, b2), and Factor C (e.g., Irrigation: c1, c2) are fully crossed to produce 2 × 2 × 2 = 8 treatment combinations. These eight combinations (a1b1c1, a1b1c2, a1b2c1, etc.) are randomized independently within each block, so every combination appears exactly once per block. Each block represents a homogeneous group of experimental units such as a contiguous strip of field or a greenhouse bench, and the blocks are replicated across the experiment to provide the error degrees of freedom needed for hypothesis testing. Randomization within blocks ensures unbiased estimation of treatment effects while blocking removes known sources of environmental variation from the error term, increasing the sensitivity of the F-tests.

3 A working example

To make things simple and interesting, we’ll explain 3FRBD analysis step by step using a hypothetical example, so you can clearly see how it works and why it matters. Consider a field experiment conducted to evaluate the effects of three factors on rice production: Factor A Variety (V1: IR64, V2: Swarna), Factor B Nitrogen level (N1: 60 kg/ha, N2: 90 kg/ha, N3 :120 kg/ha) and Factor C Irrigation frequency (I1: Alternate wetting and drying, I2: Continuous flooding). The full factorial crossing of these three factors produces 2 × 3 × 2 = 12 treatment combinations, replicated across 3 blocks, giving a total of 36 experimental plots. Observations were recorded for four variables: yield (grain yield in kg/ha), Char1 (plant height in cm) and Char 2 (number of tillers per plant).Our aim is to test whether the three factors and their interactions produce statistically significant differences in the response variables using ANOVA. 6 Transformation

Log, square root, and arcsine transformations are often used in 3FRBD analysis to make data more normal and reduce uneven variation. Researchers can use these transformations when analyzing experimental data in RAISINS as shown in Figure 6.

Logarithmic transformation is a mathematical procedure used to convert a skewed distribution into a more symmetrical one by replacing each data point (x) with its logarithm. This technique is specifically applied to positive, continuous data where the variance is proportional to the mean, a relationship common in phenomena that exhibit multiplicative or exponential growth.

Square root transformation is a statistical method used to stabilize variance and reduce right-skewness by replacing each data point (x) with its square root. It is primarily applied to non-negative, discrete “count” data such as those following a Poisson distribution, where the variance of the data tends to increase in proportion to the mean. By compressing the upper end of the scale more significantly than the lower end, this transformation brings the data closer to a normal distribution, satisfying the homoscedasticity requirements of many parametric statistical tests.

Arcsine transformation (also known as the angular transformation) is a mathematical technique specifically designed for data expressed as proportions or percentages bounded between 0 and 1. By taking the inverse sine of the square root of the proportion, this transformation stretches the ends of the distribution near 0 and 1, where variance is naturally small. It is primarily used to achieve homoscedasticity in binomial data.

After choosing the appropriate transformation proceed to Section 7 for analysis.

7 Analysis results

Once your dataset is uploaded, click on Run Analysis, and a factorial ANOVA will be performed. Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) is a statistical technique used to test the significance of differences among population means by partitioning total variation into components attributable to Factor A, Factor B, Factor C, their two-way interactions (A×B, A×C, B×C), the three-way interaction (A×B×C), Blocks, and Error, and comparing them using the F-test see Figure 7.

Table 1 ANOVA summary

ANOVA table

In a Three-Factor Randomized Block Design (3FRBD), the analysis of variance (ANOVA) partitions the total sum of squares into sources attributable to Blocks, Factor A (Variety), Factor B (Nitrogen), Factor C (Irrigation), and all interaction terms (A×B, A×C, B×C, A×B×C), plus the Residual Error. The degrees of freedom for each source are: Blocks = r−1 (where r = number of blocks), Factor A = a−1, Factor B = b−1, Factor C = c−1, A×B = (a−1)(b−1), A×C = (a−1)(c−1), B×C = (b−1)(c−1), A×B×C = (a−1)(b−1)(c−1), and Error = (r−1)(abc−1). Each mean square is obtained by dividing the sum of squares by its degrees of freedom, and the F-statistic for each source is the ratio of its mean square to the Error mean square. Significance is indicated by an asterisk (*) for the 5% level and two asterisks (**) for the 1% level of significance, displayed as superscripts for each corresponding F stat in the table. If the computed F value exceeds the critical value, the null hypothesis for that source is rejected, indicating a statistically significant effect or interaction.

Overview of ANOVA Results and Interpretation

  1. Treatments and Response Variables

Treatments: The independent variable or specific combination of factor levels (e.g., V2N2I2 — Swarna at 90 kg N/ha under continuous flooding) being tested to determine its influence on the results.

Response Variable: The dependent variable or specific measurement (e.g., grain yield in kg/ha) recorded to evaluate the performance of the treatment combinations.

  1. Multiple Comparisons

Post hoc Grouping: A method of using letters (a, b, c) to categorize means. Items sharing the same letter are statistically similar, while those with different letters are significantly different.

  1. ANOVA Summary

F stat: A numerical value that compares the variance between different groups to the variance within those groups; it determines if the overall differences are statistically significant.

p value: The probability that the observed differences occurred by random chance. A value below the chosen significance threshold typically indicates that the results are statistically significant.

  1. Critical Difference (CD) and Error Estimates

Critical Difference (CD): The minimum mathematical gap required between two means to declare them “significantly different” at a specific confidence level.

Standard Error (SE): A measure of the accuracy of a sample mean compared to the true population mean; it indicates how much the mean might fluctuate.

Mean Square Error (MSE): The average of the squared differences between observed values and the predicted mean; it represents the “noise” or unexplained error in the experiment.

Coefficient of Variation (CV%): A percentage that shows the level of dispersion in the data. A lower CV indicates higher precision and reliability in the experimental measurements.

Cohen’s F: A standardized measure of effect size that describes the magnitude of the experimental effect, regardless of the sample size.

7.2 Interpretation from Figure 8

Treatments are grouped using letters such as “a”, “b”, “c” to indicate statistical similarity based on pairwise comparisons. Overlapping grouping letters (e.g., ab or bc) indicate that the absolute difference between treatment combination means is less than the critical difference at the chosen level of significance, implying statistical similarity (on par). For example, if treatment combinations V1N2I2 and V2N2I2 both carry the label “a”, their yields are statistically similar at the 5% significance level. Treatment combinations with no common letters (e.g., a and c) differ significantly, providing a clear visual summary of all pairwise comparisons. The LSD test provides the critical difference (CD) value, which was used to determine significant differences between pairs of means. Treatments sharing at least one common letter within a character were considered not significantly different. The Cohen’s f values in the table quantify the magnitude of treatment effects: values below 0.10 indicate a very small effect, below 0.25 indicate a small effect, below 0.40 indicate a medium effect, and values of 0.40 or higher indicate a large effect. The observed effect sizes for grain yield and biomass traits indicate that the treatment effects are not only statistically significant but also practically relevant and biologically meaningful.

8 Multiple comparison tests

What is Post-hoc test?
    Post hoc test is a follow-up analysis, performed after finding a significant result in an overall statistical test (like ANOVA). Its purpose is to identify exactly which groups or treatments differ from each other. In other words, it helps to pinpoint where the differences lie between multiple groups, when the initial test shows that not all groups are the same.

After obtaining a significant F-value in ANOVA under 3FCRD, multiple comparison tests are employed to identify which treatment combination means differ significantly. Commonly used post hoc tests include Least Significant Difference (LSD), Tukey’s Honest Significant Difference (HSD), and Duncan’s Multiple Range Test (DMRT), each differing in their level of error control and suitability depending on the number of treatment combinations and experimental conditions (see Figure 9).

Post-hoc test

When the factorial ANOVA in a Three-Factor Completely Randomized Design (3FCRD) is significant, the following post hoc tests are commonly used for pairwise comparisons: Tukey’s Honestly Significant Difference (HSD) test, and Fisher’s Least Significant Difference (LSD) test.

Least Significant Difference (LSD)

The LSD test is the simplest post-hoc procedure, computed as:

\[LSD = t_{\alpha/2, df_e} \times \sqrt{\frac{2 \times MSE}{r}}\]

where \(t_{\alpha/2, df_e}\) is the critical t-value at significance level α with error degrees of freedom dfe, MSE is the Mean Square Error from the ANOVA table, and r is the number of replications (blocks). Two treatment combination means are declared significantly different if the absolute difference between them exceeds the LSD value. The LSD test is most appropriate when the number of treatment combinations is small and pre-planned comparisons are made.

Tukey’s Honestly Significant Difference (HSD)

Tukey’s test helps you find out exactly which pairs of treatment combination means differ significantly. It compares all possible pairs of treatment means while controlling the overall Type I error rate, so you avoid false positives when making multiple comparisons. In 3FRBD, this method works well because treatment combinations are assigned randomly within each block and the error variance is assumed homogeneous across combinations.

Duncan’s Multiple Range Test (DMRT)

After confirming significant overall differences via ANOVA, DMRT ranks the treatment combination means and calculates critical differences using the standardized range statistic (Q) and the standard error based on error variance from ANOVA. This systematic and sequential approach provides clear groupings of treatment combinations and has gained popularity in agricultural research.

Which Post hoc test to use?

The choice of the post hoc test completely relies on the researcher.

LSD is used for pairwise comparison of treatment combination means after a significant ANOVA in 3FRBD. It is most suitable when the number of treatment combinations is small and comparisons are limited, offering high sensitivity to detect differences, but it may increase Type I error when many combinations are compared. In agricultural experiments LSD is the most commonly used.

Tukey’s HSD is preferred when there are multiple treatment combinations in a balanced 3FRBD. It compares all possible treatment combination pairs while strictly controlling the family-wise error rate, making it a conservative and reliable method for multiple comparisons, especially when interactions are present.

DMRT is commonly used in agricultural experiments with several treatment combinations. It ranks treatment combination means step-wise and detects more significant differences than Tukey HSD, though it is less conservative and carries a higher risk of Type I error.

In the example for those characters, a pairwise comparison was performed to identify significant differences between treatment combinations using Least Significant Difference (LSD) test.

A bar plot compares the average values of different treatment combinations, with error bars showing variability. Look upon the loadings of each variable in the given Figure 26 and decide which PC-based index needs to be selected. 16 Preparing data in MS Excel

Open a new blank sheet in MS Excel with only one sheet included, and avoid adding any unnecessary content. The dataset should follow a column-based format, where the first column represents the Block (replicate), the second column represents the treatment combination label or, alternatively, separate columns for Factor A, Factor B, and Factor C. You can name these columns appropriately, such as “Block”, “Variety”, “Nitrogen”, “Irrigation”. All characters under study (e.g., yield, Char1 and Char 2) should be arranged in separate columns, and each treatment combination should be repeated according to the number of blocks. The file can be saved in CSV, XLS, or XLSX format, but CSV is recommended as it is lighter and enables faster loading. Ensure that there are no unwanted spaces in column names or group names. For reference, see the structure shown in Figure 28.
