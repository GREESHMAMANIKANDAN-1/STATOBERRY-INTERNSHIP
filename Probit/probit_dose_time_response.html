Probit Dose/Time Response Analysis

1Statoberry LLP, 2Department of Agricultural Statistics, Kerala Agricultural University

ABSTRACT

Probit Dose/Time Response Analysis is a statistical method used to model the relationship between a stimulus such as a dose of pesticide or duration of exposure and the probability of a binary response, such as mortality or inhibition, in a target population. Probit analysis transforms cumulative response proportions into probit units (normal equivalent deviates), linearising the sigmoid dose-response curve for reliable parameter estimation. In RAISINS you can perform Probit Dose/Time Response Analysis very easily without writing a single line of code. This tutorial will guide you through how to perform Probit analysis in RAISINS and interpret the results effectively. In addition, you will obtain publication-ready tables and plots, along with key estimates such as LD50, LC50, LT50, lethal dose/time confidence intervals, and slope parameters.

Introduction Probit Dose/Time Response Analysis
    The Probit model traces its origins to the pioneering work of Chester Ittner Bliss, an American entomologist and statistician who introduced the probit transformation in 1934 as a practical tool for analysing insecticide bioassay data. Bliss observed that mortality proportions, when plotted against log-dose, followed a characteristic sigmoid (S-shaped) curve. By transforming proportions to probit units the inverse of the normal cumulative distribution function he linearised this relationship, enabling straightforward estimation of lethal doses using linear regression techniques available at the time. His landmark paper published in Science (1934) laid the foundation for what became a cornerstone method in toxicology, pharmacology, and pest management. The method was subsequently refined and formalised by D.J. Finney in his comprehensive monograph Probit Analysis (1947, Cambridge University Press), which codified the maximum likelihood estimation procedure still used today. Probit analysis remains the standard approach for estimating LD50, LC50, LT50, and their fiducial confidence intervals in regulatory toxicology and bioassay studies worldwide.

2 Probit Dose/Time Response Analysis

Probit Dose/Time Response Analysis is a regression-based bioassay method that estimates the relationship between the log-dose (or log-time) of a treatment agent and the cumulative proportion of subjects exhibiting a defined quantal response, such as death, knockdown, or inhibition. The response proportions are transformed to probit units values derived from the inverse normal cumulative distribution function converting the sigmoid dose-response curve into a linear relationship amenable to ordinary or weighted least squares regression. This approach is most commonly applied in toxicology, pharmacology, entomology, and plant protection research to estimate key parameters such as the lethal dose 50% (LD50), lethal concentration 50% (LC50), lethal time 50% (LT50), and their associated fiducial confidence intervals. RAISINS implements maximum likelihood-based probit regression, automatically computing the probit line, slope, intercept, goodness-of-fit (chi-square heterogeneity test), and lethal dose/time estimates across user-specified probability levels. The method is applicable whenever the response variable is binary (alive/dead, inhibited/not inhibited) and multiple dose or time levels are tested against a group of subjects.

TipProbit Dose/Time Response Analysis is a maximum likelihood regression method that linearises sigmoid dose-response data by transforming cumulative response proportions to probit (normal equivalent deviate) units, enabling precise estimation of lethal dose or time at any specified probability level.

Table 1 — Probit Regression Summary

Probit regression table explained

The probit regression table produced by RAISINS summarises the fitted linear model relating log-dose (or log-time) to the probit of the response proportion. The key parameters reported are:

Slope (b): The regression coefficient indicating the rate at which the probit response increases per unit increase in log-dose. A steeper slope indicates a more homogeneous population response; a shallower slope suggests greater variation in susceptibility among individuals.

Intercept (a): The value of the probit when log-dose equals zero. Together with the slope, it defines the complete probit line: \(\text{Probit}(p) = a + b \cdot \log_{10}(\text{Dose})\).

Chi-square (χ²) heterogeneity test: A goodness-of-fit statistic that tests whether the observed probit values deviate significantly from those predicted by the fitted line. A non-significant chi-square (p > 0.05) indicates an adequate fit; a significant result suggests heterogeneity or non-linearity that may require investigation.

Degrees of freedom (df): Equal to the number of dose/time groups minus 2 (the number of estimated parameters). Used to evaluate the chi-square statistic against tabulated critical values.

Significance is indicated by an asterisk (*) for the 5% level and two asterisks (**) for the 1% level of significance.

6.1 Interpretation from Figure 5

The fitted probit regression line for the pyrethroid bioassay yields a slope of approximately 2.31 and an intercept of –1.68, indicating a moderately steep dose-response relationship. The chi-square heterogeneity test statistic (χ² = 4.12, df = 4) is non-significant (p = 0.39), confirming that the probit model fits the observed data adequately and there is no evidence of systematic departure from linearity. The positive slope confirms that mortality increases with increasing dose, as expected for a toxicant-response relationship. The steepness of the slope (b > 2) suggests that the Bemisia tabaci population is relatively homogeneous in its susceptibility to the tested insecticide.

Table 2 — Lethal Dose / Time Estimates with Fiducial Limits

Overview of lethal estimate parameters

  1. Lethal Dose / Time Estimates

LD50 / LC50 / LT50: The dose, concentration, or time required to kill (or elicit a response in) exactly 50% of the exposed population. This is the most commonly reported summary statistic from probit analysis and serves as a standard benchmark for comparing the potency or speed of action of different agents.

LD90 / LC90 / LT90: The dose, concentration, or time required to kill 90% of the population. This parameter is especially important in pest management and public health applications, where high levels of control are required.

LD10 / LC10 / LT10: The dose, concentration, or time eliciting a response in only 10% of subjects. Useful as a no-observed-adverse-effect benchmark in risk assessment.

  1. Fiducial Confidence Limits

Lower 95% Fiducial Limit: The lower bound of the 95% fiducial confidence interval for the lethal estimate, computed using the Fieller’s theorem approach as implemented in standard probit analysis. These limits are asymmetric on the original scale.

Upper 95% Fiducial Limit: The upper bound of the confidence interval. Together, the lower and upper fiducial limits define the range within which the true lethal value is expected to lie with 95% confidence.

  1. Slope and Relative Potency

Slope (SE): The slope of the probit line with its standard error. A steeper slope (larger b) indicates a sharper transition from no-response to full-response, reflecting a more uniform population.

Relative Potency: When two agents are compared on the same probit plot, the ratio of their LD50 estimates gives the relative potency a standardised index of comparative efficacy.

6.2 Interpretation from Figure 6

Based on the fitted probit model for the pyrethroid bioassay, the estimated LD50 is 1.82 mg/L (95% fiducial limits: 1.31–2.45 mg/L), indicating that a concentration of approximately 1.82 mg/L of the synthetic pyrethroid is required to kill 50% of the Bemisia tabaci population under the experimental conditions. The LD90 is estimated at 8.74 mg/L (95% fiducial limits: 6.12–14.20 mg/L), highlighting the substantially higher dose needed to achieve near-complete control. The narrow fiducial confidence intervals for LD50 relative to LD90 are typical and reflect greater statistical precision at the central portion of the dose-response curve. These estimates can be used to benchmark the tested insecticide against other compounds or to calculate application rates for field deployment.

TipThe fiducial confidence limits for LD50 are computed using Fieller’s theorem, which accounts for the uncertainty in both the slope and intercept of the fitted probit line they are not equivalent to simple symmetric confidence intervals based on standard errors alone.

In our example, the LD50 of 1.82 mg/L provides a reliable estimate of the potency of the tested insecticide. When comparing multiple insecticides, the compound with the lower LD50 is considered more potent it achieves the same level of control at a lower dose. The ratio of LD50 values between two compounds gives the relative potency ratio, a useful comparative index in toxicological and resistance monitoring studies.

TipCohen’s f (effect size) and R² are also reported alongside the probit regression output in RAISINS to quantify the overall strength of the dose-response relationship, independent of sample size.

7 Multiple Comparison Tests

What is a comparison test in probit analysis?

In Probit Dose/Time Response Analysis, pairwise comparisons between two or more tested agents or between different strains or populations are made by examining whether their LD50 estimates (or other lethal estimates) overlap in their 95% fiducial confidence intervals. Non-overlapping confidence intervals at the same probability level are interpreted as evidence of a statistically significant difference in potency or susceptibility. Additionally, formal significance testing can be conducted by computing the ratio of LD50 estimates and checking whether its fiducial interval includes 1.0; if the interval does not include 1.0, the two estimates are significantly different at the corresponding significance level.

In probit analysis, the primary method for comparing the potency of two or more agents or the susceptibility of two or more populations is the comparison of lethal estimates and their fiducial confidence intervals. RAISINS presents these estimates in a consolidated table (see Figure 6), making it straightforward to assess whether observed differences in LD50 or LT50 values between groups are statistically meaningful.

The three most relevant comparative metrics provided by RAISINS for probit analysis are the fiducial confidence interval overlap approach, the resistance ratio (RR), and the slope comparison.

The fiducial confidence interval overlap approach is the simplest and most widely used method in bioassay practice. If the 95% fiducial confidence intervals of two LD50 estimates do not overlap, the two potencies are considered significantly different at the 5% significance level. While this is a conservative criterion, it is robust and widely accepted in published toxicological literature.

The Resistance Ratio (RR) is computed as the ratio of the LD50 of a potentially resistant strain to the LD50 of a reference (susceptible) strain: \[RR = \frac{LD50_{\text{resistant}}}{LD50_{\text{susceptible}}}\] An RR close to 1 indicates equal susceptibility, values between 2 and 5 suggest low resistance, 5 to 10 moderate resistance, and values exceeding 10 indicate high resistance, according to standard entomological resistance classification guidelines. RAISINS reports the RR and its fiducial limits automatically when two datasets are analysed simultaneously.

The slope comparison examines whether the probit lines of two agents or populations are parallel. If the slopes are not significantly different (parallel lines), the two agents have a consistent relative potency across all dose levels and a single relative potency estimate is valid. If slopes differ significantly, the relative potency itself varies with dose level, and comparisons should be made at a specific dose level of practical relevance rather than using a single summary ratio.

From Figure 9 to Figure 13, you can see the different types of plots available in RAISINS for Probit Dose/Time Response Analysis. Each one is visually illustrated and accompanied by a clear, insightful description below, making it easy to understand what each plot conveys.

A dose-response curve in probit analysis is a graph that shows the relationship between the dose of a substance and the biological response produced. The dose is plotted on the X-axis (usually logarithmic scale) and the probit mortality or response on the Y-axis. It helps determine the effective or lethal dose, such as LD₅₀, by converting sigmoid response data into a straight line for easy interpretation.

A Probit transform plot is a graphical representation where percentage response data are converted into probit values and plotted against the logarithm of dose or concentration. This transformation changes the sigmoid dose-response curve into a straight line, making it easier to estimate values like LD₅₀ or ED₅₀ and analyze the relationship between dose and response.

A residual plot is a graph that displays residuals (differences between observed and predicted values) on the Y-axis against fitted values or independent variables on the X-axis. It is used to check model assumptions and assess goodness of fit; random scatter around zero indicates a good model fit.

An LD/LC estimate plot is a graphical representation used in toxicity studies to estimate lethal dose (LD) or lethal concentration (LC) values, such as LD₅₀ or LC₅₀. It shows the relationship between dose/concentration and the percentage mortality or response. The plot helps identify the dose or concentration required to cause death or effect in 50% of the test population.

A Bootstrap distribution plot is a graph that displays the distribution of statistics obtained from repeated bootstrap samples of a dataset. It is used to estimate variability, confidence intervals, and the stability of a parameter such as the mean or median. The plot helps visualize how the statistic is distributed across resampled data.

11 Preparing data in MS Excel

Open a new blank sheet in MS Excel with only one sheet included, and avoid adding any unnecessary content. The dataset for Probit Dose/Time Response Analysis should follow a column-based format, where the first column contains the dose or time level (numeric), the second column contains the total number of subjects exposed at each replicate-dose combination, and the third column contains the number of subjects responding (e.g., dead). Additional response variable columns (e.g., knockdown count, fecundity count) may be appended for multivariate analysis. Column names must not contain spaces use underscores or dots as separators, and avoid symbols such as %, #, or &. The file can be saved in CSV, XLS, or XLSX format, but CSV is recommended for faster loading. Ensure there are no blank rows above the data. For reference, see the structure shown in Figure 15. Data can also be arranged with repeated dose values for each replicate row, as illustrated in Figure 16.

Dataset Creation Rules

1. Column Naming Convention - No spaces allowed in column names.
- Use underscores (_) or full stops (.) for separation. - Avoid symbols and special characters like %,# etc 2. Data Arrangement - Start data arrangement towards the upper-left corner.
- Ensure the row above the data is not blank. 3. Cell Management - Avoid typing or deleting in cells without data.
- If needed, select affected cells, right-click, and select Clear Contents. 4. Column Relevance - Name all columns meaningfully.
- Include only the dose/time, total, and response columns required for probit analysis; exclude any unnecessary columns.
