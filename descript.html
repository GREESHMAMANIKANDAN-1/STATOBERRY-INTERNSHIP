Introduction Descriptive Statistics

    The systematic use of numerical summaries to describe collections of observations traces back to the pioneering work of Karl Pearson

    , the British mathematician and statistician who, in the closing decades of the nineteenth century, formalised many of the concepts now central to descriptive statistics. Pearson introduced the product-moment correlation coefficient, developed the concept of the standard deviation as a measure of spread, and championed the use of frequency distributions and histograms to visualise data contributions published in his landmark series of papers in the journal Biometrika, which he founded in 1901. His contemporary Francis Galton had earlier quantified the concept of regression toward the mean while studying the inheritance of physical traits, laying the groundwork for understanding how individual observations relate to the central tendency of a population. The early twentieth century saw the consolidation of descriptive methods into a coherent framework: measures of central tendency (mean, median, and mode) were systematically defined and compared; measures of dispersion (range, variance, standard deviation, and coefficient of variation) were formalised as indispensable companions to any measure of location; and the third and fourth standardised moments skewness and kurtosis were introduced to characterise the shape of frequency distributions beyond location and spread. Together, these tools transformed raw data into interpretable summaries that could be communicated, compared across studies, and used as the entry point for inferential reasoning. Today, Descriptive Statistics remains the universal first step in any data analysis pipeline, providing the numerical and graphical context without which inferential results cannot be meaningfully understood or communicated.

2 Descriptive Statistics

Descriptive Statistics refers to the collection of quantitative methods used to summarise, organise, and describe the principal features of a dataset without making any probabilistic inferences about a larger population. Rather than drawing conclusions that extend beyond the data at hand, descriptive statistics characterise what the data actually show providing a clear and compact numerical and graphical portrait of the observations. The core measures fall into three categories: measures of central tendency (mean, median, mode), which identify the location of the typical or representative value in the distribution; measures of dispersion (range, variance, standard deviation, coefficient of variation), which quantify how spread out or variable the observations are around that centre; and measures of shape (skewness, kurtosis), which describe the symmetry and peakedness of the distribution. Descriptive statistics are applicable to virtually any domain from agricultural and biological sciences to social sciences, clinical research, quality control, and data science and serve as the mandatory first step before any inferential or multivariate analysis is conducted. They are most informative when the goal is to understand the internal structure of a single dataset, compare the distributional properties of several groups or variables, detect outliers and anomalies, verify assumptions required by subsequent tests, or communicate the main features of data to a non-specialist audience.

TipDescriptive Statistics is the systematic numerical and graphical summarisation of a dataset, encompassing measures of central tendency, dispersion, and distributional shape, providing the indispensable foundation for all subsequent statistical analysis.

3 A working example

To make things simple and interesting, we’ll explain Descriptive Statistics step by step using the celebrated Iris dataset, one of the most widely studied datasets in statistics and machine learning, so you can clearly see how the analysis works and why it matters. The Iris dataset contains measurements of 150 iris flowers drawn from three species Iris setosa, Iris versicolor, and Iris virginica with 50 flowers per species, making the dataset perfectly balanced. For each flower, four morphological measurements were recorded in centimetres: Sepal.Length (sepal length), Sepal.Width (sepal width), Petal.Length (petal length), and Petal.Width (petal width). The three species serve as the groups (Treatments) and the four floral measurements serve as the Variables whose distributional properties we wish to characterise and compare. Our aim is to describe how the four morphological traits vary within and across the three species, to detect differences in their central tendency and spread, and to assess the shape of each variable’s distribution all of which are achieved entirely through descriptive methods without requiring a formal hypothesis test. The arrangement of the data is shown in Figure 2.

Data organised in MS Excel can be directly uploaded to RAISINS for analysis. For more details on data preparation see Section 4. Two terms that we will use frequently are Treatments and Variables. In our example, the Treatments refer to the three iris species (Iris setosa, Iris versicolor, and Iris virginica), and the Variables are the four floral traits,Sepal.Length, Sepal.Width, Petal.Length, and Petal.Width.

6 Analysis results

Once your dataset is uploaded, click on Run Analysis, and Descriptive Statistics will be computed for each variable across each species group. The primary output is a comprehensive Descriptive Statistics table that reports, for each combination of species and variable, the mean, standard deviation, standard error, minimum, maximum, coefficient of variation, skewness, and kurtosis,see Figure 5. ##Table 1 — Descriptive Statistics

Descriptive Statistics table

In a Descriptive Statistics analysis, the summary table organises all key metrics for every variable across every group in a single, readable layout. Each row corresponds to a species–variable combination, and the columns report the following quantities. The Mean is the arithmetic average of all observations within the group for that variable, representing the central location of the data. The Standard Deviation (SD) measures average deviation from the mean; a large SD relative to the mean indicates high variability. The Standard Error (SE) is the estimated precision of the group mean and is computed as SD divided by the square root of the group sample size. The Minimum (Min) and Maximum (Max) are the smallest and largest observed values, together defining the observed range of the variable in that group. The Coefficient of Variation (CV%) expresses the SD as a percentage of the mean, enabling comparison of relative variability across variables measured on different scales. Skewness quantifies asymmetry: a value near zero indicates approximate symmetry, positive values indicate a right-skewed distribution with a long upper tail, and negative values indicate left skew. Kurtosis measures the tailedness of the distribution relative to a normal curve; values substantially above 3 (excess kurtosis > 0) indicate heavier tails and a sharper peak, while values below 3 indicate a flatter, lighter-tailed distribution.

6.1 Interpretation from Figure 5

The descriptive statistics of the Iris dataset reveal clear morphological differences among the three species, setosa, versicolor, and virginica based on sepal and petal measurements. For Sepal.Length, virginica shows the highest mean value (≈ 6.59 cm) along with the largest range (3.0 cm), indicating greater variability in flower size, whereas setosa records the smallest mean (≈ 5.01 cm) with comparatively low dispersion (SD ≈ 0.35 cm). Versicolor occupies an intermediate position with a mean Sepal.Length of approximately 5.94 cm. In terms of Sepal.Width, setosa exhibits the highest average width (≈ 3.43 cm), while versicolor has the lowest mean value (≈ 2.77 cm), suggesting noticeable variation in floral structure across species.

Petal characteristics provide the most distinct separation among species. Setosa possesses the smallest Petal.Length (≈ 1.46 cm) and Petal.Width (≈ 0.25 cm) with very low standard deviations, indicating high uniformity within the species. Versicolor displays intermediate petal dimensions, with mean Petal.Length and Petal.Width values of approximately 4.26 cm and 1.33 cm respectively. Virginica exhibits the largest petals overall, with mean Petal.Length around 5.55 cm and Petal.Width around 2.03 cm, accompanied by relatively higher variability. Quartile measures (Q1, Q3, and IQR) also confirm a gradual increase in petal size from setosa to versicolor and finally to virginica.

The skewness values for most variables remain close to zero, indicating approximately symmetric distributions, although slight positive and negative skewness are observed in certain petal and sepal measurements. Kurtosis values are generally near the normal reference value of 3, implying moderate distribution shapes without extreme peakedness or flatness. Overall, the descriptive statistics indicate that petal-related variables are the most effective features for distinguishing among the three Iris species and therefore play a major role in classification and predictive modeling studies.

##Table 2: Normality Test

Overview of Descriptive Statistics Results and Interpretation

Normality tests are used to determine whether a dataset follows a normal distribution, which is essential for selecting appropriate statistical methods. In this study, the Shapiro–Wilk test and Anderson–Darling test were applied to assess the normality of four important iris dataset variables: Petal Length, Petal Width, Sepal Length, and Sepal Width. Descriptive statistical analysis was also performed to summarize the characteristics of the three species Iris setosa, Iris versicolor, and Iris virginica using measures such as mean, median, standard deviation, range, minimum, and maximum values. These measures helped explain the central tendency and variability of the floral traits. In addition, the Chi-square test was conducted to examine associations and distributional differences among the species. The combined analysis provided a clear understanding of variation, relationships, and statistical patterns within the iris dataset.

6.2 Interpretation from Figure 6

The Shapiro–Wilk normality test results for the Iris dataset indicate that most of the selected variables follow an approximately normal distribution across the three species. For Iris setosa, Sepal.Length and Sepal.Width exhibit high p-values (0.46 and 0.27 respectively), suggesting no significant deviation from normality. Petal.Length also remains close to normality with a p-value of 0.05, whereas Petal.Width shows a statistically significant departure from normality (p ≈ 0.00), indicating a non-normal distribution for this variable within the species.

For Iris versicolor, Sepal.Length, Sepal.Width, and Petal.Length all satisfy the normality assumption with p-values greater than 0.05, reflecting approximately symmetric and bell-shaped distributions. However, Petal.Width records a p-value of 0.03, suggesting that this variable does not follow a normal distribution for versicolor.

In the case of Iris virginica, all four variables Sepal.Length, Sepal.Width, Petal.Length, and Petal.Width display p-values above the 0.05 significance threshold, indicating that the distributions are consistent with normality. The W-statistics for most variables remain close to 1, further supporting the presence of near-normal distributions.

Overall, the normality analysis demonstrates that the majority of sepal and petal measurements satisfy the assumption of normality, with the exception of Petal.Width in setosa and versicolor. These findings suggest that parametric statistical methods can generally be applied to the Iris dataset, although caution may be required for variables exhibiting non-normal behavior.

TipWhen comparing groups using descriptive statistics, always report both a measure of central tendency (mean or median) and a measure of dispersion (SD or CV%) together , the mean alone does not convey whether the groups are internally consistent or highly variable.
TipA low CV% (below 10–15%) within a group indicates high measurement precision and internal homogeneity; a high CV% may signal natural biological variability, measurement error, or the presence of outliers that warrant further investigation.

A density plot is a graphical representation used in statistics to show the distribution and concentration of continuous data values. It provides a smooth curve that estimates the probability density of a dataset, helping to identify patterns such as skewness, peaks, spread, and variation more clearly than a traditional histogram in data analysis.

A histogram plot is a graphical representation used to display the frequency distribution of numerical data. It groups data into continuous intervals called bins and uses adjacent bars to show how often values occur within each interval. Histograms help in understanding data distribution, central tendency, spread, and patterns such as skewness or symmetry

A box plot compares the distribution of each morphological measurement across the three Iris species. Each coloured box represents one species and shows key statistics: the median (middle line), the interquartile range (the box itself), and potential outliers (points outside the whiskers). The species-level separation is immediately visible for petal variables, where the three boxes are non-overlapping, while sepal variables show greater overlap between versicolor and virginica.

A ridgeline plot is a data visualization method used to display multiple distributions simultaneously through overlapping density curves. Each curve represents a different group or category, arranged one above another for easy comparison. Ridgeline plots help identify patterns, variations, trends, and differences in distributions across several datasets clearly and effectively.

A correlogram plot is a graphical representation used to display the correlation matrix between multiple variables in a dataset. It uses colors, shapes, or sizes to indicate the strength and direction of correlations. Correlograms help in identifying relationships, patterns, and dependencies among variables for better statistical analysis and interpretation.

A bar plot is a graphical representation used to compare different categories of data using rectangular bars. The length or height of each bar represents the value or frequency of the category. Bar plots help in visualizing comparisons, trends, and differences among groups clearly and effectively in statistical analysis.

A beeswarm plot is a data visualization technique used to display individual data points without overlap. Points are arranged closely along a categorical axis to show the distribution, density, and spread of data clearly. It helps in identifying clusters, outliers, and variation while preserving the visibility of every observation.

12 Preparing data in MS Excel

Open a new blank sheet in MS Excel with only one sheet included, and avoid adding any unnecessary content. The dataset should follow a column-based format, where the first column represents the group or species label you can name this column appropriately, such as “Species” or “Group.” All variables under study (e.g., Sepal.Length, Sepal.Width, Petal.Length, Petal.Width) should be arranged in separate columns, and each observation should occupy a single row. The file can be saved in CSV, XLS, or XLSX format, but CSV is recommended as it is lighter and enables faster loading. Ensure that there are no unwanted spaces in column names or group names. For reference, see the structure shown in Figure 24. As illustrated in Figure 2, groups must appear as labels in one column repeated across all observations belonging to that group, and the data can also be arranged as shown in Figure 25.

Dataset Creation Rules

1. Column Naming Convention - No spaces allowed in column names.
- Use underscores (_) or full stops (.) for separation. - Avoid symbols and special characters like %,# etc 2. Data Arrangement - Start data arrangement towards the upper-left corner.
- Ensure the row above the data is not blank. 3. Cell Management - Avoid typing or deleting in cells without data.
- If needed, select affected cells, right-click, and select Clear Contents. 4. Column Relevance - Name all columns meaningfully.
- Exclude unnecessary columns not required for analysis.

How to Save as CSV in MS Excel

1. Open Your Workbook

-   Ensure your data is arranged properly with only one sheet.

  1. Click ‘File’ Menu

    • Go to the top-left corner and click on File.

  2. Choose ‘Save As’ or ‘Save a Copy’

    • Select the location where you want to save your file.

  3. Set File Type to CSV

    • In the ‘Save as type’ dropdown menu, choose CSV (Comma delimited) (*.csv).

  4. Name Your File

    • Enter a relevant file name without spaces (use underscores if needed).

  5. Click ‘Save’

    • Click Save to export the file.
