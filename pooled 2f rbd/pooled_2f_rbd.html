Pooled Two-Factor Randomised Block Design

1Statoberry LLP, 2Department of Agricultural Statistics, Kerala Agricultural University

ABSTRACT

Pooled Two-Factor Randomised Block Design (Pooled 2F-RBD) is a factorial experimental design that combines the error-control advantages of randomised blocking with a two-factor treatment structure, enabling researchers to simultaneously examine the main effects of two factors and their interaction while eliminating the influence of a known source of variation through grouping of experimental units into homogeneous blocks. Pooled 2F-RBD partitions total variation into components attributable to Blocks, Factor A, Factor B, the A×B interaction, and the pooled error — where the interaction sum of squares is pooled with the block-by-treatment residual when the interaction is non-significant — thereby increasing the precision and power of main-effect tests in field and greenhouse experiments. In RAISINS you can perform Pooled 2F-RBD very easily without writing a single line of code. This tutorial will guide you, how to perform Pooled 2F-RBD very easily in RAISINS and interpret the results effectively. In addition, you will get tables and plots ready for publication. You can also perform a multivariate analysis including MANOVA and PCA.

Introduction Pooled Two-Factor Randomised Block Design
    The theoretical foundation of the Randomised Block Design was laid by Sir Ronald A. Fisher during his pioneering work at Rothamsted Experimental Station in the 1920s. Fisher recognised that in field experiments, soil fertility, moisture, and other environmental gradients introduce systematic variation that inflates experimental error and reduces the power of F-tests. By grouping experimental units into homogeneous blocks — each block containing one unit per treatment — he demonstrated that block-to-block variation could be removed from the error term, yielding a more sensitive and unbiased comparison of treatments. The extension of this principle to factorial treatment structures, in which two or more factors are studied simultaneously at all combinations of their levels, followed naturally from Fisher’s broader advocacy of factorial experimentation articulated in his 1935 monograph The Design of Experiments. The two-factor RBD became the workhorse of field crop and horticultural research through the mid-twentieth century, valued both for its error-control efficiency and for its ability to reveal interaction effects between factors. The pooled variant of this design, in which the non-significant A×B interaction sum of squares is merged into the residual error, was formalised as computational experience accumulated showing that when the true interaction is negligible, pooling increases the degrees of freedom available for error estimation and thereby produces more powerful F-tests for the main effects of Factor A and Factor B. Today, the Pooled 2F-RBD is a standard analytical framework in agronomy, plant breeding, soil science, and allied biological disciplines wherever field heterogeneity warrants blocking and two factors are of simultaneous scientific interest.

2 Pooled Two-Factor Randomised Block Design (Pooled 2F-RBD)

Pooled Two-Factor Randomised Block Design (Pooled 2F-RBD) is a factorial experimental design in which two factors — each at two or more levels — are studied across all possible level combinations, and the resulting treatment combinations are assigned to experimental units arranged in homogeneous blocks, with each block containing exactly one unit for every treatment combination. The “randomised block” feature ensures that any systematic variation present in the experimental material — such as a soil fertility gradient across a field, a light gradient in a greenhouse, or batch effects in a laboratory — is absorbed into the block sum of squares and removed from the error term, thereby increasing the sensitivity of the F-tests for the factorial effects. The design is termed “pooled” because, when the A×B interaction is found to be statistically non-significant, the interaction sum of squares and its associated degrees of freedom are merged with the residual (within-blocks) error to form a pooled error term, providing more degrees of freedom for error estimation and further strengthening the main-effect tests. The ANOVA for a Pooled 2F-RBD partitions total variation into five components: Blocks (variation due to differences among blocks), Factor A (main effect of the first factor), Factor B (main effect of the second factor), A×B Interaction (joint effect beyond additive main effects), and Pooled Error (residual variation after pooling when interaction is non-significant). This design is most appropriate when experimental units can be grouped into blocks that are internally more homogeneous than the experiment as a whole, and it is preferred over the Pooled 2F-CRD whenever a meaningful source of nuisance variation can be identified and used as a blocking criterion. When no such gradient exists, the Pooled 2F-CRD is the simpler and equally valid alternative.

Pooled 2F-RBD Layout

    Figure 2 visually represents a Pooled 2F-RBD arrangement with two factors — Factor A at a levels and Factor B at b levels — forming a total of a × b treatment combinations, each appearing exactly once within each of r blocks. The a × b × r experimental units are organised so that each block constitutes one complete replication of all treatment combinations, and within each block the combinations are assigned entirely at random to the available units. In the pooled version, when the A×B interaction F-test is non-significant, the interaction sum of squares ((a − 1)(b − 1) df) is pooled with the residual error ((ab − 1)(r − 1) df less the interaction df) to yield a single pooled error term with more degrees of freedom, which is then used for all F-tests on main effects and for computing critical differences in post-hoc comparisons.

3 A working example

To make things simple and interesting, we’ll explain Pooled 2F-RBD analysis step by step using a hypothetical example, so you can clearly see how it works and why it matters. Consider a field experiment conducted to evaluate the effect of two factors on wheat grain yield and quality: Factor A — Fertiliser dose at 3 levels (F1: 60 kg N/ha, F2: 90 kg N/ha, F3: 120 kg N/ha) and Factor B — Sowing method at 2 levels (S1: Line sowing, S2: Broadcast sowing), resulting in 3 × 2 = 6 treatment combinations (F1S1, F1S2, F2S1, F2S2, F3S1, F3S2). The experiment is laid out in 4 blocks, each block representing a strip of the field with relatively uniform soil fertility, giving a total of 6 × 4 = 24 experimental plots, with all 6 treatment combinations randomised independently within each block. The response variables recorded were: Yield (grain yield in kg/ha), Obs1 (number of grains per spike), Obs2 (spike length in cm), and FW (1000-grain weight in g). Our aim is to test whether Fertiliser dose, Sowing method, and their interaction produce statistically significant differences in the response variables using ANOVA under Pooled 2F-RBD. The arrangement of the data is shown in Figure 3.

In our example, the Treatments refer to the 6 fertiliser-by-sowing combinations (F1S1 through F3S2), and the Variables are the 4 traits mentioned earlier — Yield, Obs1, Obs2, FW.

6 Transformation

Log, square root, and arcsine transformations are often used in Pooled 2F-RBD analysis to make data more normal and to stabilize variance before the factorial ANOVA is carried out. Researchers can use these transformations when analyzing experimental data in RAISINS as shown in Figure 6.

Logarithmic transformation is a mathematical procedure used to convert a skewed distribution into a more symmetrical one by replacing each data point (x) with its logarithm. This technique is specifically applied to positive, continuous data where the variance is proportional to the mean, a relationship common in phenomena that exhibit multiplicative or exponential growth.

Square root transformation is a statistical method used to stabilize variance and reduce right-skewness by replacing each data point (x) with its square root. It is primarily applied to non-negative, discrete “count” data such as those following a Poisson distribution, where the variance of the data tends to increase in proportion to the mean. By compressing the upper end of the scale more significantly than the lower end, this transformation brings the data closer to a normal distribution, satisfying the homoscedasticity requirements of many parametric statistical tests.

Arcsine transformation (also known as the angular transformation) is a mathematical technique specifically designed for data expressed as proportions or percentages bounded between 0 and 1. By taking the inverse sine of the square root of the proportion, this transformation stretches the ends of the distribution near 0 and 1, where variance is naturally small. It is primarily used to achieve homoscedasticity in binomial data.

After choosing the appropriate transformation proceed to Section 7 for analysis.

7 Analysis results

Once your dataset is uploaded, click on Run Analysis, and the Pooled 2F-RBD ANOVA will be performed. The analysis first evaluates the A×B interaction effect. If the interaction is non-significant, the interaction sum of squares and degrees of freedom are pooled with the within-blocks residual error to form the pooled error term. The ANOVA then partitions total variation into components attributable to Blocks, Factor A (Fertiliser dose), Factor B (Sowing method), and Pooled Error, and tests each main effect using the F-ratio of its mean square to the pooled error mean square (see Figure 7). A significant main effect indicates that the levels of that factor produce meaningfully different responses on average across all levels of the other factor and across all blocks.

Pooled 2F-RBD ANOVA table

In a Pooled Two-Factor Randomised Block Design (Pooled 2F-RBD), the ANOVA partitions total variation into the following sources of variation:

Blocks — variation due to differences among the r blocks used to control field heterogeneity, with degrees of freedom = r − 1. The block mean square is not tested against the pooled error in the standard analysis; its removal from the error term is the primary purpose of blocking.

Factor A — the main effect of the first factor (e.g., Fertiliser dose), with degrees of freedom = a − 1, where a is the number of levels of Factor A.

Factor B — the main effect of the second factor (e.g., Sowing method), with degrees of freedom = b − 1, where b is the number of levels of Factor B.

A×B Interaction — the joint effect of Factors A and B beyond their additive contributions, with degrees of freedom = (a − 1)(b − 1). When this term is non-significant, it is pooled into the residual error.

Pooled Error — the combined residual (within-blocks) error and (when non-significant) the interaction sum of squares. The pooled degrees of freedom = (r − 1)(ab − 1) − (a − 1)(b − 1) + (a − 1)(b − 1) = (r − 1)(ab − 1) before pooling, with the interaction df added upon pooling. In practice this equals (ab − 1)(r − 1) + (a − 1)(b − 1) after merging.

Each mean square is obtained by dividing its sum of squares by its degrees of freedom. The F-statistic for each main effect is the ratio of its mean square to the pooled error mean square. Significance is indicated by an asterisk (*) for the 5% level and two asterisks (**) for the 1% level. A significant F-test for a main effect confirms that the levels of that factor differ significantly in their effect on the response variable, averaged across all levels of the other factor and across all blocks.

Overview of Pooled 2F-RBD Results and Interpretation

  1. Treatments and Response Variables

Factor A: The first independent variable (e.g., Fertiliser dose) being tested to determine its main effect on the response variable, averaged across all levels of Factor B and all blocks.

Factor B: The second independent variable (e.g., Sowing method) being tested to determine its main effect and its interaction with Factor A.

Response Variable: The dependent variable or specific measurement (e.g., grain yield in kg/ha) recorded to evaluate the performance of the treatment combinations.

  1. Multiple Comparisons

Post-hoc Grouping: A method of using letters (a, b, c) to categorize treatment means. Items sharing the same letter are statistically similar, while those with different letters are significantly different. In Pooled 2F-RBD, post-hoc tests are applied separately for Factor A means and Factor B means when main effects are significant and the interaction is non-significant.

  1. Pooled 2F-RBD Summary

F stat: A numerical value that compares the variance attributable to a factor or interaction to the pooled residual variance, determining whether the effect is statistically significant.

p value: The probability that the observed differences occurred by random chance. A value below the chosen significance threshold indicates statistically significant effects.

  1. Critical Difference (CD) and Error Estimates

Critical Difference (CD): The minimum mathematical gap required between two means to declare them “significantly different” at a specific confidence level, computed using the pooled error mean square.

Standard Error (SE): A measure of the precision of a treatment combination mean; it reflects residual variability after removing block effects and pooling the interaction and residual errors.

Mean Square Error (MSE): The pooled error mean square from the Pooled 2F-RBD ANOVA table. It is smaller than the unpooled residual error when the non-significant interaction variance is incorporated, confirming the efficiency gain from pooling.

Coefficient of Variation (CV%): A percentage that shows the level of dispersion in the data relative to the grand mean. A lower CV confirms higher experimental precision, and values below 20% are generally considered acceptable for field experiments.

Cohen’s F: A standardized measure of effect size that describes the magnitude of the factor effect, independent of sample size.

7.2 Interpretation from Figure 8

Treatment combination means and Factor-level means are grouped using letters such as “a”, “b”, “c” to indicate statistical similarity based on pairwise comparisons using the pooled error mean square. Overlapping grouping letters (e.g., ab or bc) indicate that the absolute difference between two means is less than the critical difference at the chosen significance level, implying statistical similarity (on par). For example, among the three Fertiliser dose levels, if F3 (120 kg N/ha) is labelled “a” and F1 (60 kg N/ha) is labelled “c”, it indicates that F3 produces a significantly higher grain yield than F1. For Sowing method, if S1 (Line sowing) carries label “a” and S2 (Broadcast sowing) carries label “b”, the two sowing methods differ significantly in their effect on yield. Since the interaction is non-significant, these main-effect comparisons are valid and interpretable independently of the other factor. The Cohen’s f values quantify the magnitude of each factor’s effect: values below 0.10 indicate a very small effect, below 0.25 a small effect, below 0.40 a medium effect, and values of 0.40 or higher a large effect.

8 Multiple comparison tests

What is Post-hoc test?
    Post-hoc test is a follow-up analysis, performed after finding a significant result in an overall statistical test (like ANOVA). Its purpose is to identify exactly which groups or treatments differ from each other. In other words, it helps to pinpoint where the differences lie between multiple groups, when the initial test shows that not all groups are the same. In Pooled 2F-RBD, post-hoc tests are applied to the levels of each significant main effect using the pooled error mean square and pooled error degrees of freedom, ensuring that the additional precision gained from both the blocking and the pooling of the non-significant interaction is fully exploited in pairwise comparisons.

After obtaining a significant F-value for a main effect in the Pooled 2F-RBD, multiple comparison tests are employed to identify which factor-level means differ significantly. The same post-hoc procedures used in one-way RBD analysis — LSD, Tukey’s HSD, and DMRT — are applied, but they use the pooled error mean square and the pooled error degrees of freedom, thereby benefiting from the increased precision associated with both blocking and pooling (see Figure 9).

Post-hoc test

When the Pooled 2F-RBD ANOVA is significant for a main effect, the following post-hoc tests are commonly used for pairwise comparisons of factor-level means.

LSD (Least Significant Difference) Test

The Least Significant Difference (LSD) test identifies which specific factor-level means differ significantly after the pooled ANOVA has confirmed an overall significant main effect. The LSD is calculated using the pooled error mean square:

\[\text{LSD} = t_{\alpha/2, \, df_{\text{pooled error}}} \sqrt{\frac{2 \, \text{MSE}_{\text{pooled}}}{r \cdot b}}\]

for Factor A comparisons, where t₍α/2, df_pooled error₎ is the critical t-value at the chosen significance level, MSEpooled is the pooled error mean square, r is the number of blocks, and b is the number of levels of Factor B. An analogous formula applies for Factor B comparisons, substituting the number of levels of Factor A (a) in place of b. Any absolute difference between two factor-level means exceeding this LSD value is declared statistically significant.

Tukey’s Honestly Significant Difference (HSD)

Tukey’s test identifies which pairs of factor-level means differ significantly while controlling the overall Type I error rate. It is computed using the pooled error mean square and pooled error degrees of freedom, and is especially appropriate when the number of levels of a factor is moderate to large and all pairwise comparisons are of interest.

Duncan’s Multiple Range Test (DMRT)

After confirming a significant main effect via the pooled ANOVA, DMRT ranks the factor-level means and calculates sequential critical differences based on the pooled error mean square. It provides clear grouping letters for factor-level means and is widely used in field crop and agronomy experiment reporting where a concise summary of treatment rankings is required.

Which Post-hoc test to use?

The choice of the post-hoc test completely relies on the researcher.

LSD is used for pairwise comparison of factor-level means after a significant main effect in Pooled 2F-RBD. It is most suitable when the number of levels is small and comparisons are pre-planned, offering high sensitivity to detect differences after pooling and blocking. In agricultural and biological factorial field experiments, LSD is the most commonly used post-hoc test.

Tukey’s HSD is preferred when there are four or more levels of a factor in a balanced Pooled 2F-RBD. It compares all possible pairs of factor-level means while strictly controlling the family-wise error rate, making it a conservative and reliable method for multiple comparisons of pooled-error-adjusted means derived from blocked experiments.

DMRT is commonly used in field crop experiments with several factor levels and provides step-wise critical differences for ranking factor-level means. It detects more significant differences than Tukey HSD, though it is less conservative and carries a higher risk of Type I error.

In the example for those characters, a pairwise comparison of factor-level means was performed using the Least Significant Difference (LSD) test.

This plot is especially informative in Pooled 2F-RBD when the interaction is non-significant and the researcher wishes to visualise the additive progression of treatment combination means across the multivariate response surface.

14 Multivariate

Multivariate analysis in Pooled 2F-RBD helps you to compare different response variables simultaneously across all treatment combinations using the factorial blocked dataset. Remember that the PCA used for multivariate selection is an exploratory technique, not an inferential method. Now, in our example of evaluating the effect of Fertiliser dose (F1, F2, F3) and Sowing method (S1, S2) on wheat grain quality traits Yield, Obs1, Obs2, and FW navigate to Multivariate, see Figure 20.

PCA will be automatically carried out based on the selected variables. PCA results and plots will appear along with automated interpretation.

The scree plot given Figure 22 illustrates the proportion of variance explained by each principal component.

Look upon the loadings of each variable in the given Figure 23 and decide which PC-based index needs to be selected. In PC1, Yield and Obs1 (grains per spike) show high positive loadings (approximately +0.63 and +0.57 respectively), while FW (1000-grain weight) also loads positively (+0.44) on PC1, indicating that treatment combinations with high scores on PC1 are superior in both grain yield and grain number. Obs2 (spike length) loads more prominently on PC2 (+0.69), suggesting it captures a relatively independent dimension of variation in response to the fertiliser-sowing combination. Based on this pattern, a PC1-based index score is most informative for identifying fertiliser-by-sowing combinations that maximise grain yield and grain filling performance simultaneously. It is recommended to use variables that are highly correlated for PCA, as this helps in constructing a more reliable and meaningful index.

The biplot gives a visual representation of the relationships among the fertiliser-by-sowing treatment combinations and the measured response variables. Treatment combinations positioned in the direction of a variable vector tend to exhibit higher values for that particular trait. The angle between variables in the biplot indicates their correlation — smaller angles suggest high positive correlation, while larger angles close to 90 degrees suggest weak or no correlation. Combinations such as F3S1 and F3S2 are typically positioned toward the positive PC1 direction, reflecting their overall superiority in yield-related traits, while combinations involving F1 are positioned toward the negative PC1 direction, consistent with their lower yields under minimal nitrogen application.

In RAISINS, we calculate a scaled index score by converting the index score to a range of 0 to 1, making it easier to interpret and compare. This standardized approach ensures consistency in evaluating treatment combinations based on their index scores. The default cutoff is set at 75%. By toggling the up-arrow and down-arrow buttons below the cutoff selection, you can select the top or bottom percentage of treatment combinations as per your preference. 16 Preparing data in MS Excel

Open a new blank sheet in MS Excel with only one sheet included, and avoid adding any unnecessary content. The dataset for Pooled 2F-RBD must follow a column-based format with at least four structural columns: a Block column (identifying the block number for each observation, e.g., 1, 2, 3, 4), a Factor A column (identifying the level of the first factor, e.g., Fertiliser dose for each observation), a Factor B column (identifying the level of the second factor, e.g., Sowing method for each observation), and one or more columns for the response variables Yield, Obs1, Obs2, FW. Each row represents one experimental plot, and the repeated appearances of each factor-level combination across blocks represent the block replications. Ensure that every treatment combination appears exactly r times (4 times in our example, once per block), and that the order of rows need not be sorted — RAISINS identifies the combinations from the Block, Factor A, and Factor B column entries automatically. The file can be saved in CSV, XLS, or XLSX format, but CSV is recommended as it is lighter and enables faster loading. Ensure that there are no unwanted spaces in column names or group names. For reference, see the structure shown in Figure 26. The data can also be arranged as shown in Figure 27.

Dataset Creation Rules

1. Column Naming Convention - No spaces allowed in column names.
- Use underscores (_) or full stops (.) for separation. - Avoid symbols and special characters like %,# etc 2. Data Arrangement - Start data arrangement towards the upper-left corner.
- Ensure the row above the data is not blank. 3. Cell Management - Avoid typing or deleting in cells without data.
- If needed, select affected cells, right-click, and select Clear Contents. 4. Column Relevance - Name all columns meaningfully.
- Exclude unnecessary columns not required for analysis.
